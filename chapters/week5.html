6  Colonial Data Legacies


6.1 Goal

The Western scientific tradition has developed in a world structured in part by colonial injustices. This module attends to the consequent influence of colonial power dynamics on the structure and use of research data and data repositories.

6.2 Readings

  • Raja et al., 2021: “Colonial history and global economics distort our understanding of deep-time biodiversity”
  • Monarrez et al., 2021: “Our past creates our present: a brief overview of racism and colonialism in Western paleontology”
  • TallBear, 2007: “Narratives of race and indigeneity in the genographic project”
  • Radin, 2017: “‘Digital Natives’: How Medical and Indigenous Histories Matter for Big Data”

6.3 Questions

  • How are scientific data entangled in histories of settler colonialism and empire?
  • These readings collectively posit a relationship wherein both societal injustices influence the production of scientific knowledge, and scientific knowledge contributes to the perpetuation of those injustices? How exactly does this reciprocal relationship work, according to the readings?
  • What ethical problems do these papers raise, and what solutions do they offer, if any?
  • Do your own data bear the trace of colonial history or other injustices? How, if at all, have you attempted to repair the injustice which they reinscribe?
